Commutator-Graph Term Ordering Reduces First-Order Trotter Error at a Fixed Gate Budget

The order of the per-term rotations in a first-order Lie–Trotter product formula is a free parameter: it changes the algorithmic error but not the gate count. We read an ordering off the commutator (anticommutation) graph of \(H=\sum_j c_j P_j\) and report—transparently, with confidence intervals—exactly how large and how robust the effect is against exact statevector references.

Background & motivation

Digital quantum simulation of \(e^{-iHt}\) by product formulas is the canonical near-term primitive. For a Pauli decomposition \(H=\sum_{j=1}^{L} c_j P_j\), the first-order (Lie–Trotter) approximant with \(r\) steps and an ordering \(\pi\) has its error controlled by the commutators of the non-commuting summands (the commutator-scaling analysis of Childs et al.). Two facts set up the gap we address.

Cost is order-independent

A first-order schedule applies one rotation \(e^{-ic_jP_jt/r}\) per term per step, so the gate count is \(L\cdot r\) regardless of the order \(\pi\) in which the rotations are applied.

Error is order-dependent

The leading correction is a sum over commutators \([c_aP_a,c_bP_b]\), which vanishes for any commuting pair and is sensitive to which non-commuting terms sit adjacently in the product. Ordering is therefore a cost-free control parameter.

The precise gap

Ordering sensitivity has been observed empirically in chemistry Trotterization, but without a graph-centric formalization, a matched-cost exact evaluation, and an transparent, statistically reported quantification of how large and how robust the effect is.

Unlike higher-order formulas, randomized compilation, or step-size selection—which change the gate budget or the algorithm—reordering changes nothing about the circuit. Any fidelity it recovers is pure profit. The structural object that exposes which pairs contribute to the leading error is the same commutator graph used for measurement grouping in variational algorithms, repurposed here for time evolution.

Tutorial and primer for research scientists

Hamiltonian simulation approximates \(e^{-iHt}\) by applying one small rotation per Pauli term. The number of rotations is fixed once \(L\), \(r\), and the Hamiltonian are fixed, but the order of those rotations is still a design choice. This project asks how much fidelity can be recovered by choosing that order from the commutator graph, while keeping the gate proxy unchanged.

Input

A Pauli Hamiltonian \(H=\sum_j c_jP_j\) from structured TFIM, Heisenberg, or molecular-like families.

Representation

A commutator graph whose nodes are Hamiltonian terms and whose edges mark anticommuting pairs, weighted by coefficient structure.

Scoring

Exact-reference statevector fidelity, infidelity, observable error, anticommuting adjacency count, and matched gate proxy \(Lr\).

Statistical highlight

The reported-scale benchmark uses 120 structured Hamiltonians and 95% confidence intervals. The clear effect is commutator-aware ordering versus random; differences among non-random heuristics are treated cautiously when they fall within noise.

Novelty highlight

The contribution is a graph formalization of a zero-cost ordering mechanism, with exact-reference evaluation and an interpretable learned router that tracks, rather than overclaims to surpass, the best fixed strategy.

Contributions & novelty

The contribution is a clean formalization and a rigorous measurement of a zero-cost mechanism—not a fundamentally new primitive, and positioned as such. Three contributions, exactly as stated in the manuscript:

  • 1A graph formalization of first-order Trotter ordering. We cast term ordering as minimization of the coefficient-weighted count of anticommuting adjacencies on the commutator graph \(\mathcal{G}(H)\), and give a greedy walk that achieves a near-minimal count at zero change to the gate proxy \(L\cdot r\).
  • 2A matched-cost, exact, statistically reported measurement. We evaluate the effect against exact statevector references on 120 structured Hamiltonian instances and report every quantity as a mean with a 95% confidence interval, deliberately separating the statistically clear effect (ordering vs. random) from the within-noise comparison among non-random heuristics.
  • 3A small interpretable learned router. A logistic-regression classifier over eight commutator-graph features, trained leave-one-instance-out, selects among the fixed orderings. We show it tracks rather than surpasses the best fixed strategy—reported as a sanity-checked selector, not a method that exceeds the heuristics.

Stated limitations are surfaced up front: first-order only; small systems (\(n\le 8\), where an exact reference is tractable); synthetic/structured Hamiltonians rather than production molecules; a gate-count proxy \(L\cdot r\); and within-noise differences among the non-random orderings at this scale. See Conclusion & impact.

Mathematics & method

Every object below is exact and matrix-free at the inner kernel; the only dense exponential is the small reference used for cross-checking and fidelity scoring.

Hamiltonian and first-order Trotter unitary

$$H=\sum_{j=1}^{L} c_j\,P_j,\qquad P_j\in\{I,X,Y,Z\}^{\otimes n},\ c_j\in\mathbb{R}.$$

$$U_{\mathrm{Trot}}(\pi)=\Bigg(\prod_{j\in\pi} e^{-i\,c_j P_j\, t/r}\Bigg)^{\!r}.$$

The implementation cost is one rotation per term per step, \(L\cdot r\), invariant under the ordering \(\pi\).

Exact, matrix-free per-term rotation

Each Pauli is an involution, \(P^2=I\), so

$$e^{-i\theta P}\,|\psi\rangle=\cos\theta\,|\psi\rangle-i\sin\theta\,\big(P|\psi\rangle\big),$$

a single sparse application of \(P\) (a permutation-and-phase of amplitudes) plus a scaled add, in \(O(n\,2^n)\) time—never forming a \(2^n\times 2^n\) matrix.

Commutator graph and the ordering policy

Commutation is an \(O(n)\) parity test: two Pauli strings commute iff the number of qubits on which their single-qubit factors anticommute is even. The commutator graph \(\mathcal{G}(H)\) has one node per term and an edge for every anticommuting pair.

The greedy commutator ordering walks \(\mathcal{G}(H)\): starting from the largest-\(|c_j|\) term, it appends an unused term that commutes with the current tail if possible; otherwise the anticommuting term with the smallest coefficient product \(|c_{\mathrm{tail}}||c_j|\) (the cheapest unavoidable commutator). A dual view is a greedy coloring of \(\mathcal{G}(H)\) into mutually commuting groups.

Leading error ∝ adjacent anticommuting pairs

For one step \(S_\pi(\Delta)=\prod_a e^{-i\Delta h_{\pi(a)}}\) (\(h_j=c_jP_j\), \(\Delta=t/r\)):

For Pauli terms \(\|[c_jP_j,c_kP_k]\|\) is \(0\) when the strings commute and \(2|c_jc_k|\) when they anticommute. The weighted anticommutation graph is the leading-order object controlling the ordering-dependent error; the schedule-level surrogate the policy minimizes is the coefficient-weighted count of adjacent anticommuting pairs.

Experimental design & statistics

The reported-scale configuration (configs/full.yaml, seed 0) is designed so that any fidelity difference is attributable solely to ordering: every ordering is evaluated at an identical gate budget against an exact statevector reference.

Sample (n = 120 instances)
3 families × 5 sizes \(n\in\{4,5,6,7,8\}\) × 8 seeded draws. Per-family statistics use the \(N=40\) instances per family.
Families
Transverse-field Ising \(H=-J\sum_i Z_iZ_{i+1}-h\sum_i X_i\); Heisenberg \(H=\sum_i(J_xX_iX_{i+1}+J_yY_iY_{i+1}+J_zZ_iZ_{i+1})\); and random molecular-like local 2-body Pauli terms (a tractable stand-in for mapped electronic-structure Hamiltonians).
Matched gate budget
Cost is the proxy \(L\cdot r\) (one rotation per term per step), invariant under reordering and reported alongside every comparison. Fixed point: \(t=2.0\), \(r=3\), mean proxy = 48.
95% confidence interval
Reported as the half-width \(1.96\,\hat\sigma/\sqrt{N}\) over instances (\(\hat\sigma\) = sample standard deviation). Frontier bands use the same definition per point.
Cross-verification (integrity gate)
The fast Pauli-rotation kernel is checked against dense scipy.linalg.expm to tolerance \(10^{-9}\) on random Paulis before any fidelity is emitted; the run records pauli_algebra_verified = true. The exact reference \(e^{-iHt}|\psi_0\rangle\) (probe state \(|+\rangle^{\otimes n}\)) is itself cross-checked against Krylov expm_multiply.
Provenance & audit
All numbers derive from a single results/summary.json with full provenance; an automated audit (make audit) verifies every reported number traces to a generated artifact and rejects forbidden superiority phrasing. The reported run completed in ≈100 s with ≈406 MB peak memory.

The clear effect: ordering vs. random

Random ordering reaches mean fidelity 0.205 ± 0.040; every non-random ordering more than doubles it, with the commutator ordering at 0.548 ± 0.068. The intervals are disjoint and the 0.343 mean fidelity gain spans many CI widths. Equivalently, infidelity falls from 0.795 to 0.452—a 1.76× reduction at the identical proxy of 48. The mechanism is visible in the adjacency count: commutator ordering leaves only 0.28 adjacent anticommuting pairs vs 3.08 (random) and 6.47 (locality).

The transparent caveat: within-noise among non-random orderings

The commutator (0.548), coefficient (0.550), and learned (0.549) means lie within 0.002 of one another—far inside their ≈±0.067 intervals—and are not statistically distinguishable at this scale. The defensible statement: commutator ordering is among the best strategies and reaches that with by far the fewest anticommuting adjacencies (a directly interpretable, ordering-only surrogate); we do not claim it significantly surpasses the coefficient heuristic on fidelity here, and the learned router tracks rather than surpasses the best fixed strategy.

Gate-budget frontier (\(r\in\{1,2,3,4,6,8,12\}\))

Random ordering is dominated at every budget on the curve. At mean proxy 38 (\(r=3\)) random reaches 0.193 while commutator and coefficient reach 0.548 and 0.550; at proxy 76 (\(r=6\)) the values are 0.589 (random) vs 0.823 (commutator). All orderings converge toward unit fidelity as \(r\) grows—ordering confers error reduction at a fixed budget, not a change in the asymptotic rate.

Per-family breakdown (\(N=40\) each)

Heisenberg: every non-random ordering reaches 1.000 ± 0.000 vs random 0.169 ± 0.086—the clearest case. Transverse-field Ising: non-random orderings (≈0.480 ± 0.049) roughly double random (0.287 ± 0.051); locality is worst (0.350). Random molecular-like: all orderings are indistinguishable (0.15–0.17)—an transparent negative result: on dense, generic graphs at small \(r\) the ordering mechanism is exhausted.

Conclusion & impact

Term ordering is a cost-free control parameter on first-order Trotter error. A commutator-graph ordering reaches the high-fidelity end of the achievable range while minimizing the anticommuting adjacencies that generate the leading error—more than doubling mean fidelity relative to random at an identical gate budget across structured Hamiltonians.

What the result is

A statistically clear, reproducible finding that ordering materially changes first-order Trotter fidelity at zero extra gate cost, with disjoint confidence intervals between ordered and random. For near-term, budget-limited regimes, recovered fidelity is pure profit.

Transparent limitations

First-order only; small systems (\(n\le 8\)); synthetic/structured families; a gate-count proxy \(L\cdot r\); within-noise differences among non-random orderings; the effect attenuates on dense molecular-like graphs at small \(r\); classical CPU simulation; solo author. Hardware validation is left for future work.

Outlook

Natural next steps: higher-order (Suzuki) formulas, larger systems with scalable (tensor-network / Krylov) references, real mapped molecular Hamiltonians, and a graph neural ordering policy trained directly on a differentiable Trotter-error objective.
