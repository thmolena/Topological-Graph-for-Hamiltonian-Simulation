Commutator-Graph Term Ordering Reduces First-Order Trotter Error at a Fixed Gate Budget

The term order in a first-order Lie–Trotter product formula is a free parameter: the per-step gate count is fixed at one rotation per Pauli term, yet the algorithmic error depends on the order through the commutators of non-commuting summands. Treating the terms as nodes of a commutator graph and scheduling them to minimize coefficient-weighted anticommuting adjacencies recovers substantial fidelity at zero additional gate cost.

Background and Motivation

The scientific setting and the open structural question that this work resolves for the first-order regime.

Simulating the time evolution $e^{-\mathrm{i}Ht}$ of a quantum many-body Hamiltonian is a canonical application of quantum computers and the original motivation for the field (Feynman 1982; Lloyd 1996). The most widely deployed simulation primitive is the product formula. For a Hamiltonian in its Pauli decomposition $H=\sum_{j=1}^{L} c_j P_j$, the first-order (Lie–Trotter) approximant with $r$ steps and term ordering $\pi$ is $U_{\mathrm{Trot}}(\pi)=\big(\prod_{j\in\pi} e^{-\mathrm{i}\,c_j P_j\, t/r}\big)^{r}$. Its error against the exact propagator is governed by the commutators of the summands; the modern tight analysis (Childs et al., Theory of Trotter Error with Commutator Scaling, 2021) makes the dependence on nested commutators explicit and is the theoretical anchor of this work.

Two facts about that schedule motivate the paper. First, the implementation cost of a first-order schedule is one rotation $e^{-\mathrm{i}c_j P_j t/r}$ per term per step, so the gate count is $L\!\cdot\!r$ independent of the ordering $\pi$. Second, the error does depend on $\pi$, because the leading commutator sum is sensitive to which non-commuting terms are placed adjacently. The ordering is therefore a free knob that can lower Trotter error at no extra gate cost. While higher-order Suzuki formulas, randomized compilation, and step-size selection are the usual routes to cheaper simulation, those change either the gate budget or the algorithm; reordering does not. The open question addressed here is how large, and how statistically robust, the ordering-only effect is across structured Hamiltonian families under an exact, matched-cost evaluation.

Problem Formulation

The precise statement that the benchmark instantiates.

Fix a Hamiltonian $H=\sum_{j=1}^L c_j P_j$, an evolution time $t$, and a step count $r$. By construction every term ordering $\pi\in S_L$ produces the same gate-budget proxy $L\!\cdot\!r$ (one single-Pauli rotation per term per step). The task is to choose $\pi$ to maximize the first-order Trotter fidelity $\mathcal{F}(\pi)=\big|\langle\psi_{\mathrm{exact}}|\psi_{\mathrm{Trot}}(\pi)\rangle\big|^2$ against the exact propagator, at this identical cost. Because the leading first-order error is, to order $t^2/r$, the coefficient-weighted sum over the anticommuting edges of $\mathcal{G}(H)$ that fall adjacent in $\pi$, the optimization reduces to an ordering-only combinatorial surrogate: minimize the coefficient-weighted number of adjacent anticommuting pairs in the schedule. The benchmark evaluates this lever across 120 structured instances (transverse-field Ising, Heisenberg, and random molecular-like families; $n\in\{4,\dots,8\}$ qubits; eight seeded draws each) against exact statevector references, and reports every quantity as a mean with a 95% confidence interval.

Principal Contributions

Three complete claims, each delimited by the manuscript's stated scope.

  • First-order Trotter term ordering is formalized as minimization of the coefficient-weighted count of anticommuting adjacencies on the commutator graph $\mathcal{G}(H)$, and a greedy walk policy is given that achieves a near-minimal count at no change to the gate budget $L\!\cdot\!r$.
  • The effect is measured against exact statevector references on 120 structured Hamiltonian instances, with every quantity reported as a mean and a 95% confidence interval, separating the statistically clear effect (ordering vs. random) from the within-noise comparison among non-random heuristics.
  • A small interpretable learned router — logistic regression over eight commutator-graph features, trained leave-one-instance-out — is shown to track the best fixed strategy rather than beat it, confirming that the graph features carry the relevant signal under a listing-order–invariant policy.

Methodological Novelty

The contribution stated against named prior art.

Versus measurement grouping

The commutator graph and its clique cover are established in measurement grouping for variational algorithms (Verteletskyi 2020; Gokhale 2020; Crawford 2021), where commuting Pauli sets reduce measurement settings. This work repurposes the same structure for time evolution ordering, not measurement.

Versus empirical ordering studies

Trotterized chemistry is known to be order-sensitive empirically (Tranter 2019; Poulin 2015). The contribution here is a graph-centric formalization with an exact matched-cost evaluation and explicit confidence intervals, not an isolated observation.

Versus Trotter-error theory

The commutator scaling of Trotter error is sharply characterized (Childs et al. 2021; 2018). This work couples that bound to an ordering-only surrogate — adjacent anticommuting pairs — that is directly minimizable and directly measurable, with an honest within-noise caveat among non-random heuristics.

Theoretical Guarantees

Key statements transcribed from the Methods, each with a one-line interpretation.

Proposition 1 — Gate-cost invariance under reordering
For every ordering $\pi\in S_L$, the first-order schedule $U_{\mathrm{Trot}}(\pi)$ applies exactly $L\!\cdot\!r$ single-Pauli rotations; the gate proxy $L\!\cdot\!r$ is independent of $\pi$.
Reordering changes no gate — any fidelity difference is attributable to ordering alone. This is the premise of the matched-cost protocol.
Lemma — Commutation is an $O(n)$ parity test
For Pauli strings $P,Q$ with $k$ anticommuting positions, $PQ=(-1)^{k}QP$; they commute iff $k$ is even. The test runs in $O(n)$ and never forms a $2^n\times 2^n$ matrix.
The commutator graph is built without exponential cost; edges are decided by a bitwise parity over $n$ positions.
Lemma — Pauli commutator value
For $h_j=c_jP_j$, $h_k=c_kP_k$: $[h_j,h_k]=0$ if $P_j,P_k$ commute, and $\|[h_j,h_k]\|=2|c_jc_k|$ if they anticommute.
Each anticommuting (graph-edge) pair contributes operator-norm weight $2|c_jc_k|$; commuting pairs contribute nothing.
Theorem — First-order ordering error proxy
With $\Delta=t/r$ and $S_\pi(\Delta)=\prod_{a=1}^L e^{-\mathrm{i}\Delta h_{\pi(a)}}$, $$\big\|e^{-\mathrm{i}tH}-S_\pi(t/r)^r\big\|\;\le\;\frac{t^2}{2r}\!\!\sum_{1\le aTo leading order the only ordering-dependent part of the error is the coefficient-weighted sum over anticommuting pairs — precisely the quantity the commutator policy minimizes, at the fixed budget of Proposition 1.
Proposition — Colouring is a commuting partition
A proper vertex colouring of $\mathcal{G}(H)$ partitions the terms into pairwise-commuting classes; each class exponentiates without first-order Trotter error among its members.
The greedy ordering has a dual reading as a commuting-group colouring, connecting it to classic commuting-group Trotter optimization.
Proposition — Listing-order invariance of the router
The learned router's choice depends only on the intrinsic weighted graph, not on the order in which terms are listed.
The router's selection is a property of the Hamiltonian, not of an incidental term enumeration — which is why it tracks the best fixed strategy.

Significance and Impact

The consequence for the field and the downstream application, with the paper's honest limitations kept visible.

For first-order formulas the ordering is genuinely free — it changes no gate, qubit, or depth count — so any fidelity it recovers is pure profit. In the intermediate-budget regime where near-term devices operate, the recovered fidelity is sizable: more than doubling mean fidelity at an identical gate budget. The same commutator graph also yields a commuting-group colouring whose colour classes can in principle be exponentiated jointly, connecting ordering to the classic commuting-group Trotter optimization and to a forward path of graph-neural ordering policies trained on a differentiable Trotter-error objective.

The honest boundaries are reported, not omitted. (i) The study is first-order (Lie–Trotter) only; the effect of ordering under higher-order Suzuki formulas is untested. (ii) Systems are small ($n\le 8$) because an exact statevector reference must be formed; the at-scale effect, requiring tensor-network or Krylov references, is not measured. (iii) Families are synthetic/structured proxies, not production mapped molecular Hamiltonians. (iv) Cost is a proxy $L\!\cdot\!r$ that ignores rotation synthesis and connectivity. (v) Among non-random orderings the fidelity differences are within the confidence intervals at this scale; only the ordering-versus-random effect and the adjacency-count reduction are robust. (vi) All results are classical CPU statevector simulations with no hardware validation, single-author. The contribution is a clean, reproducible characterization of a no-cost lever — deliberately small, first-order, and classically simulated — not a claim of hardware advantage.

Reproducibility

The pip-installable code artifact and the path to identical results.

The package topoham (CPU-only; numpy / scipy / networkx / scikit-learn / matplotlib) lives at submission/code and exposes a single end-to-end entry point. Every reported number is generated into the single source of truth results/summary.json and transcribed verbatim; an audit gate (make audit) fails the build on any number that does not trace to a generated artifact, and the Pauli-rotation kernel is cross-checked against dense scipy.linalg.expm to $10^{-9}$ before any fidelity is emitted.

# From the repository root
cd submission/code
make install         # numpy / scipy / networkx / scikit-learn / matplotlib (CPU-only)
make test            # 24 tests incl. Pauli-rotation ↔ expm cross-check to 1e-9
make full-run        # reported-scale config: 120 instances → results/summary.json
make tables figures  # results/main_results.{tex,md}, figures/*.pdf
make audit           # readiness gate: traceable numbers, no forbidden claims

# Or, after `pip install -e .` in submission/code:
topoham-reproduce --config configs/full.yaml

Determined entirely by seeded draws; the reported scale (configs/full.yaml) is 120 instances across three families and five sizes $n\in\{4,\dots,8\}$ with eight seeds each. Runtime and memory are reported per benchmark; all experiments run on commodity CPU hardware. The interactive illustration above re-implements the same exact single-Pauli rotation in vanilla JavaScript, so the page math matches the Python package.
