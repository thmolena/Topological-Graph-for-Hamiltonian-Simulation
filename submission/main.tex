\begin{abstract}
The order in which the terms of a Hamiltonian $H=\sum_j c_j P_j$ are applied in a
first-order (Lie--Trotter) product formula is a free parameter at fixed gate
cost. We show that this freedom is far larger, and far more useful, than a single
ordering: the lever is a per-step \emph{schedule}, and the right schedule, not
the right ordering, is what reduces error. We make the claim exact through the
\emph{commutator error form}---the leading-order Trotter error operator
$E_\pi=\sum_{a<b}[h_{\pi(a)},h_{\pi(b)}]$ assembled matrix-free as a signed sum of
Pauli strings, validated against dense matrix exponentials to $10^{-9}$.
Reordering only \emph{re-signs} the commutators in $E_\pi$; on the structured
families we study it leaves the leading-error operator norm within a factor
$\ImpMedian$ of its minimum, and \emph{exactly invariant} whenever the pairwise
commutators are distinct. No fixed ordering can therefore escape the $O(t^2/r)$
first-order rate (Theorem~\ref{thm:impotence})---an ordering-impotence result that
explains, rather than laments, why ordering heuristics are empirically within
noise. A step-alternating \emph{antithetic} schedule instead cancels $E_\pi$
\emph{exactly} (Theorem~\ref{thm:antithetic}), reaching $O(t^3/r^2)$ accuracy at
the \emph{identical} $L\cdot r$ rotation count. Across $\Ninstances$ structured
Hamiltonian instances (transverse-field Ising, Heisenberg, and random
molecular-like families, $4$--$8$ qubits) evaluated against exact statevector
references, the antithetic schedule raises the empirical convergence rate from
$\SlopeFirst$ to $\SlopeAnti$ and reaches fidelity $0.99$ at $\GateSpeedup\times$
fewer rotations than the best first-order ordering, while first-order Trotter does
not reach $0.999$ within the swept budget and the schedules do. A learned router
over ten commutator-graph features selects the gate-optimal schedule per instance
with $\LearnedAcc\%$ leave-one-out accuracy, recovering near-oracle gate
efficiency. All results are classical, fully reproducible statevector simulations
generated by an installable package.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Simulating the time evolution $e^{-\ii H t}$ of a quantum many-body Hamiltonian is
one of the canonical applications of quantum computers and the original motivation
for the field~\cite{feynman1982simulating,lloyd1996universal}. The most widely
deployed simulation primitive is the product formula. For a Hamiltonian written in
its Pauli decomposition,
\begin{equation}
    H=\sum_{j=1}^{L} c_j P_j ,
    \label{eq:ham}
\end{equation}
with real coefficients $c_j$ and Pauli strings $P_j\in\{I,X,Y,Z\}^{\otimes n}$,
the first-order (Lie--Trotter) approximant with $r$ steps and a term ordering $\pi$
is
\begin{equation}
    U_{\mathrm{Trot}}(\pi)=\Bigg(\prod_{j\in\pi} e^{-\ii\,c_j P_j\, t/r}\Bigg)^{\!r}.
    \label{eq:trotter}
\end{equation}
Its error against the exact propagator is controlled by the commutators of the
summands: the leading correction to a single step is
$\tfrac{t^2}{2r}\sum_{a<b}[c_a P_a,\, c_b P_b]$, which vanishes for any pair of
terms that commute~\cite{trotter1959product,suzuki1991general,childs2021theory}.
The modern, tight analysis of this error---``Theory of Trotter Error with
Commutator Scaling''---makes the dependence on nested commutators explicit and is
the theoretical anchor of this
work~\cite{childs2021theory,childs2018toward}.

The \emph{implementation cost} of a first-order schedule is one rotation $e^{-\ii
c_j P_j t/r}$ per term per step, so the gate count is $L\cdot r$ \emph{independent
of the ordering} $\pi$. The error does depend on $\pi$. This makes the ordering a
free knob, and a substantial body of empirical work has asked how much it
buys~\cite{tranter2019ordering,poulin2015trotter,hastings2015improving}. The
structural object that exposes which pairs of terms contribute is the
\emph{commutator graph} (or anticommutation graph): one node per term, with an
edge between two terms whenever they anticommute. This is the same graph used in
measurement grouping for variational algorithms, where a clique cover into
mutually commuting sets reduces the number of measurement
settings~\cite{verteletskyi2020measurement,gokhale2020optimization,crawford2021efficient}.

This paper begins from a sharp, and initially negative, observation: \emph{at a
fixed gate budget a single term ordering is a weak lever}, and we can say exactly
why. We introduce the \emph{commutator error form}, the exact leading-order error
operator
\begin{equation}
    E_\pi=\sum_{a<b}\bigl[h_{\pi(a)},h_{\pi(b)}\bigr],\qquad h_j=c_jP_j,
    \label{eq:errorform}
\end{equation}
and show it can be assembled without ever forming a $2^n\times2^n$ matrix, as a
signed sum of Pauli strings indexed by the edges of the commutator graph
(Methods). Reordering the schedule only flips the signs of the individual
commutators in Eq.~\eqref{eq:errorform}; it never changes which commutators
appear. The Hilbert--Schmidt norm of $E_\pi$ is therefore \emph{exactly}
ordering-invariant whenever the edge commutators land on distinct Pauli strings,
and only the small ``colliding'' fraction is ordering-reducible at all
(Theorem~\ref{thm:impotence}). Empirically the leading-error operator norm of a
random ordering sits within a factor $\ImpMedian$ of the per-instance minimum.
\emph{No fixed ordering escapes the $O(t^2/r)$ first-order rate.} This is the
precise, provable form of the ``ordering is within noise'' folklore.

The resolution is to enlarge the free degree of freedom. The order applied at
each Trotter step need not be the same; the genuinely free object is a per-step
\emph{schedule} $\sigma=(\pi_1,\dots,\pi_r)$ at the identical $L\cdot r$ rotation
count. Among schedules there is one that cancels Eq.~\eqref{eq:errorform}
\emph{exactly}: the \emph{antithetic} schedule that alternates a term order with
its reverse on successive steps. Each adjacent step-pair is then a palindromic,
and hence symmetric, product formula whose leading $O(t^2/r)$ commutator error
vanishes, upgrading the convergence from $O(t^2/r)$ to $O(t^3/r^2)$ at \emph{no
change in gate count} (Theorem~\ref{thm:antithetic}). The mechanism is the
classical symmetrisation of Suzuki~\cite{suzuki1991general,hatano2005finding}, but
its reading as a zero-overhead \emph{ordering} choice---one that the
impotence theorem shows a single fixed ordering can never realise---is, to our
knowledge, new, and it changes the practical recommendation for first-order
simulation.

\paragraph{Contributions and scope.}
We make four contributions. (i) We define the commutator error form
(Eq.~\eqref{eq:errorform}), give an exact matrix-free construction validated to
$10^{-9}$, and prove an \emph{ordering-impotence theorem}: a single ordering
cannot reduce the leading-error norm below an instance floor that is exactly
ordering-invariant on collision-free families (Theorem~\ref{thm:impotence}). (ii)
We prove that the antithetic schedule cancels the leading error and converges at
second order at the identical gate budget (Theorem~\ref{thm:antithetic}), and we
combine it with a commuting-clique compression of the commutator graph
(Proposition~\ref{prop:colour}). (iii) We measure these effects against exact
statevector references on $\Ninstances$ structured Hamiltonian instances, reporting
the matched-cost efficiency metric---rotations to reach a target
fidelity---and the empirical convergence rate, with the schedule reaching $0.99$
at $\GateSpeedup\times$ fewer rotations and $0.999$ where first-order Trotter does
not reach it at all. (iv) We add an interpretable learned router over ten
commutator-graph features that selects the gate-optimal schedule per instance and
recovers near-oracle efficiency. We state the boundaries plainly: the study is
classical statevector simulation on small systems ($n\le 8$) where an exact
reference is tractable, on synthetic/structured Hamiltonians, with a rotation-count
cost model; the gains are asymptotic (small step size) and we report the
crossover budget honestly.

\subsection{Ordering is provably impotent}
\label{sec:impotence}

The leading first-order error operator is Eq.~\eqref{eq:errorform}. For Pauli
terms an anticommuting pair contributes $[c_jP_j,c_kP_k]=2c_jc_k\,P_jP_k$, a phased
Pauli string, and a commuting pair contributes zero, so $E_\pi$ is a signed sum
over the edges of $\calG(H)$,
\begin{equation}
    E_\pi=\sum_{\{j,k\}\in E(\calG)}\varepsilon_{jk}(\pi)\,2c_jc_k\,(P_jP_k),
    \label{eq:edgesum}
\end{equation}
with $\varepsilon_{jk}(\pi)=+1$ if $j$ precedes $k$ in $\pi$ and $-1$ otherwise
(Methods). Reordering the schedule changes only the signs $\varepsilon_{jk}$. If
two distinct edges never produce the same Pauli string $P_jP_k$ (a
\emph{collision}), the Hilbert--Schmidt norm of $E_\pi$ is a sum of fixed-magnitude
terms and is therefore \emph{identical for every ordering}; only colliding edges
make any part of the error ordering-reducible (Theorem~\ref{thm:impotence}).

Table~\ref{tab:impotence} reports the collision structure: on the transverse-field
Ising and Heisenberg families the anticommuting pairs produce \emph{no} collisions
($\IrrTFIM$ and $\IrrHeis$ of the leading error is ordering-invariant), and even on
the dense molecular-like family the ordering-invariant fraction is $\IrrMol$.
Figure~\ref{fig:impotence}a quantifies the consequence directly: sampling
orderings per instance and forming the exact leading-error \emph{spectral} norm,
the coefficient ordering sits within a factor $\ImpCoeff$ of the per-instance
minimum on average ($\ImpMedian$ for a median random ordering), with a worst case
of $\ImpMax$ across all $\Ninstances$ instances. The matrix-free Hilbert--Schmidt
surrogate moves even less (within $\ImpHS$). \emph{A single ordering cannot escape
the first-order rate}: this is the precise content of the empirical observation
that ordering heuristics are statistically indistinguishable, and it motivates
enlarging the search space from orderings to schedules.

\subsection{A free schedule cancels the leading error}
\label{sec:antithetic}

The antithetic schedule applies a term order $\pi$ on even steps and its reverse
$\pi^{R}$ on odd steps. Each adjacent step-pair is a palindromic product formula;
by the standard symmetric-formula argument its leading $O(t^2/r)$ commutator error
cancels, so the schedule converges at second order---$O(t^3/r^2)$---at the
\emph{identical} $L\cdot r$ rotation count (Theorem~\ref{thm:antithetic}). This is
not an asymptotic curiosity: Figure~\ref{fig:convergence} shows the empirical
convergence rate directly. Fitting the log--log slope of mean infidelity against
the step count $r$, the three first-order schedules cluster at rate $\SlopeFirst$
(the curves lie nearly on top of one another, a visual proof of impotence), while
the antithetic and symmetric folds achieve rate $\SlopeAnti$ and $\SlopeSym$
respectively---a clean doubling of the convergence order. The antithetic curve
shares the first-order rotation count exactly; only its per-step direction differs.

\subsection{Matched-cost efficiency: rotations to a target fidelity}
\label{sec:frontier}

The practically decisive quantity is the number of rotations needed to reach a
target accuracy. Figure~\ref{fig:frontier} plots infidelity against the realised
rotation count for every schedule; the second-order folds dominate the entire
high-accuracy region. Because the convergence rates differ, the gap \emph{widens}
with budget: the folds reach fidelity $0.99$ and $0.999$ well to the left of the
first-order schedules, which plateau. Table~\ref{tab:gates} reports the crossing
points. To reach fidelity $0.99$ the best first-order ordering needs $\GtFirst$
rotations, the antithetic schedule $\GtAnti$ and the symmetric schedule $\GtSym$---a
$\GateSpeedup\times$ reduction. To reach $0.999$, \emph{no} first-order schedule
succeeds within the swept budget (entry ``\GtFirstHard''), while the antithetic and
symmetric schedules reach it at $\GtAntiHard$ and $\GtSymHard$ rotations. The free
antithetic schedule, costing exactly $L\cdot r$ rotations, tracks the
$2\times$-per-step symmetric fold on this matched-cost axis to within line width.

\subsection{The effect is family-dependent and learnable}
\label{sec:family}

Figure~\ref{fig:family} and Table~\ref{tab:family} decompose the rotations-to-target
by family. On the dense \emph{molecular-like} and \emph{transverse-field Ising}
families the folded schedules cut the cost to reach $0.99$ by roughly a third to a
half and are the only schedules to reach $0.999$. On \emph{Heisenberg}, the
commuting-clique structure makes the first-order clique schedule essentially
\emph{exact} at a single step (Proposition~\ref{prop:colour}): every clique-based
schedule reaches $0.999$ at the minimal rotation count, and the symmetric fold's
$2\times$ per-step overhead is, here, wasted. The gate-optimal schedule is thus
genuinely instance-dependent: fold the hard instances, and leave the
fast-forwardable ones first-order.

A small, interpretable learned router exploits exactly this structure. A
standardised logistic-regression classifier over ten listing-order-invariant
features of $\calG(H)$ (term count, anticommutation density, mean/max degree,
number of colour cliques, largest-clique fraction, coefficient variation, mean
Pauli weight, and two commutator-error-form features---the colliding and
ordering-invariant fractions) predicts, per instance, which fixed schedule reaches
the target at the fewest rotations. Trained leave-one-instance-out (no test
instance informs its own predictor), it attains $\LearnedAcc\%$ accuracy against
the per-instance oracle. On the subset of $\CommonN$ instances where every fixed
schedule reaches the target, the router needs $\LearnedMeanGates$ rotations on
average against an oracle optimum of $\OracleMeanGates$ and the best single fixed
schedule's $\SymMeanGates$ (symmetric) and $\AntiMeanGates$ (antithetic): it
recovers near-oracle efficiency by routing fast-forwardable instances to the cheap
first-order schedule and hard instances to a fold. The router is interpretable and
listing-order invariant by construction (Proposition~\ref{prop:invariance}).

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{What the result is, and is not.}
The defensible, reproducible finding has two halves that must be kept together.
\emph{Negatively}, a single term ordering is a provably weak lever for first-order
Trotterisation: reordering only re-signs the leading-error commutators, the
leading-error norm is exactly invariant on collision-free families and within a few
percent of its floor otherwise, and no ordering escapes the $O(t^2/r)$ rate. This
turns the well-known empirical observation that ordering heuristics are within
noise into a theorem. \emph{Positively}, the freedom that does matter is the
per-step schedule: the antithetic fold cancels the leading error and converges at
second order at the identical gate count, reaching a target fidelity at
$\GateSpeedup\times$ fewer rotations and reaching $0.999$ where first-order Trotter
plateaus. We do not claim a new product-formula \emph{primitive}: the
symmetrisation mechanism is classical~\cite{suzuki1991general,hatano2005finding}.
We claim the framing---that the lever is the schedule, that a fixed ordering
provably cannot realise it, and that the commutator graph both certifies the
impotence and structures the schedule---together with the matched-cost
quantification and the learned selector.

\paragraph{Relation to prior work.}
The commutator graph and its clique structure are established in measurement
grouping for variational algorithms~\cite{verteletskyi2020measurement,gokhale2020optimization,crawford2021efficient};
we repurpose them for \emph{time evolution} and, beyond ordering, for scheduling.
The sensitivity of Trotterised chemistry to term ordering has been reported
empirically~\cite{tranter2019ordering,poulin2015trotter,hastings2015improving}; the
impotence theorem explains the ceiling those studies encounter. Symmetric (Strang)
and higher-order product formulas are classical~\cite{suzuki1991general,hatano2005finding},
and randomised compilation---random permutation per step and
qDRIFT---improves error scaling by averaging rather than
cancellation~\cite{childs2019faster,campbell2019random}; the antithetic schedule is
the \emph{deterministic, zero-variance} counterpart that cancels the same leading
term in a single sample, and our matched-cost frontier places it against both the
first-order and the randomised baselines. The tight commutator-scaling theory of
Trotter error~\cite{childs2021theory,childs2018toward} is the lens throughout; our
commutator error form is its explicit, matrix-free, ordering-resolved
representation.

\paragraph{Why a matched-cost schedule matters.}
For first-order formulas the schedule is genuinely free---it changes no gate, qubit,
or depth count---so any accuracy it recovers is pure profit. In the high-accuracy
regime where precise chemistry and materials simulation operate, the difference
between an $O(t^2/r)$ and an $O(t^3/r^2)$ method at fixed cost is the difference
between reaching and not reaching a target, as the unreachable $0.999$ column of
Table~\ref{tab:gates} shows. The commutator graph supplies both halves of the
recommendation at no extra cost: it certifies when ordering cannot help, and its
clique colouring tells the learned router which instances to fold and which to
leave alone.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item \textbf{Asymptotic gains with an honest crossover.} The second-order
      advantage is a small-step-size effect: at coarse steps the antithetic fold
      can be \emph{worse} than a good first-order ordering (its curve in
      Figure~\ref{fig:frontier} starts above and crosses below around the
      intermediate budget). We report the crossover rather than hide it; the gains
      are in the high-accuracy regime.
    \item \textbf{Small systems, exact reference.} The exact statevector reference
      requires forming $e^{-\ii Ht}$, limiting us to $n\le8$ qubits. The effect at
      scale, where a tensor-network or Krylov reference is needed, is not measured;
      the convergence-rate argument is, however, size-independent.
    \item \textbf{Synthetic / structured Hamiltonians.} The families are
      transverse-field Ising, Heisenberg, and random local two-body
      molecular-like sums---a proxy for, not an instance of, mapped molecular
      electronic-structure Hamiltonians at scale~\cite{bravyi2002fermionic,whitfield2011simulation}.
    \item \textbf{Rotation-count cost model.} The cost is the realised rotation
      count and ignores the gate-synthesis cost of individual rotations and
      hardware connectivity; the antithetic schedule's seam rotations could be
      merged to reduce the count further, which we do not exploit so that its
      budget matches first-order exactly.
    \item \textbf{Classical simulation; solo author.} All results are classical
      statevector simulations on CPU; there is no hardware validation, and the
      work is single-author.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Conclusion.}
The free parameter in first-order Trotterisation is not the ordering but the
schedule. A single ordering is provably impotent---it only re-signs the leading
commutator error---whereas a step-alternating antithetic schedule cancels that
error and converges at second order at the identical gate budget, reaching target
fidelities that first-order Trotter cannot. The commutator graph certifies the
impotence and structures the schedule, and a small learned router recovers
near-oracle gate efficiency by folding only the instances that need it. The natural
next steps are larger systems with scalable references, real mapped molecular
Hamiltonians, higher-order folded schedules, and a graph neural policy trained
directly on a differentiable matched-cost objective. All experiments are
reproducible on commodity hardware; runtime and memory are reported for each
benchmark.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

This section gives the formal development. The Pauli algebra
(Sec.~\ref{sec:pauli}) yields the exact matrix-free simulator and the commutator
error form; Sec.~\ref{sec:graph_method} states and proves cost invariance, the
ordering-impotence theorem, the antithetic-cancellation theorem, the clique
exactness, and the listing-order invariance of the learned router. All theorems
are stated under explicitly named hypotheses with complete proofs.

\subsection{Pauli algebra and the exact statevector simulator}
\label{sec:pauli}
A Pauli string $P\in\{I,X,Y,Z\}^{\otimes n}$ acts on $\ket{\psi}\in\C^{2^n}$. We
write $\{A,B\}=AB+BA$ and $[A,B]=AB-BA$. The single-qubit Paulis satisfy
$X^2=Y^2=Z^2=I$ and $\{X,Y\}=\{Y,Z\}=\{Z,X\}=0$. We record the structural facts
that make an exact matrix-free simulator possible, each with a complete proof.

\begin{lemma}[Commutation is an $O(n)$ parity test]
\label{lem:parity}
Let $P=\bigotimes_{q=1}^{n}p_q$ and $Q=\bigotimes_{q=1}^{n}q_q$ be Pauli strings.
Call position $q$ \emph{anticommuting} if $p_q\neq q_q$ and neither is $I$, and let
$k=k(P,Q)$ be the number of anticommuting positions. Then $PQ=(-1)^{k}QP$, so $P$
and $Q$ commute iff $k$ is even. The test runs in $O(n)$ time and forms no
$2^n\times2^n$ matrix.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
At each position either $p_q=q_q$ or one is $I$ (then $p_qq_q=q_qp_q$), or $p_q\neq
q_q$ and neither is $I$ (then $p_qq_q=-q_qp_q$ by $\{X,Y\}=\{Y,Z\}=\{Z,X\}=0$). So
$p_qq_q=(-1)^{\epsilon_q}q_qp_q$ with $\epsilon_q=1$ exactly on anticommuting
positions. As the tensor product factorises across qubits,
$PQ=\bigotimes_q p_qq_q=\bigl(\prod_q(-1)^{\epsilon_q}\bigr)\bigotimes_q q_qp_q
=(-1)^{k}QP$, $k=\sum_q\epsilon_q$. Hence $PQ=QP$ iff $k$ even.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Pauli rotation as a cosine--sine pair]
\label{lem:rotation}
For a non-identity Pauli string $P$ ($P^2=I$) and $\theta\in\R$,
\begin{equation}
    e^{-\ii\theta P}=\cos\theta\,I-\ii\sin\theta\,P,
    \qquad
    e^{-\ii\theta P}\ket{\psi}=\cos\theta\,\ket{\psi}-\ii\sin\theta\,P\ket{\psi}.
    \label{eq:rotation}
\end{equation}
The right-hand side is one sparse application of $P$ plus a scaled vector add,
computable in $O(n\,2^n)$ time without forming the $2^n\times2^n$ matrix.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Since $P^{2m}=I$ and $P^{2m+1}=P$, regrouping the absolutely convergent series of
$e^{-\ii\theta P}$ by parity gives $I\sum_m(-1)^m\theta^{2m}/(2m)!
-\ii P\sum_m(-1)^m\theta^{2m+1}/(2m+1)!=\cos\theta\,I-\ii\sin\theta\,P$. A single
Pauli string maps each basis state to one basis state with a unit-modulus phase, so
$P\ket{\psi}$ costs $O(n\,2^n)$.
\end{proof}

Before any fidelity number is produced, Eq.~\eqref{eq:rotation} is cross-checked
against dense \texttt{scipy.linalg.expm} on random small Paulis to tolerance
$10^{-9}$ (the \texttt{pauli\_algebra\_verified} flag). The product of two Pauli
strings is itself a phased Pauli, $P\,Q=\omega\,R$ with $\omega\in\{\pm1,\pm\ii\}$
and $R$ a Pauli string, computed in $O(n)$ (the \texttt{pauli\_product} kernel);
this is the algebraic basis of the commutator error form below.

\subsection{Cost invariance, ordering impotence, and antithetic cancellation}
\label{sec:graph_method}
The commutator graph $\calG(H)$ has one node per term and an edge $\{j,k\}$ for
every anticommuting pair (Lemma~\ref{lem:parity}). A \emph{schedule} assigns to each
of the $r$ Trotter steps a permutation of $\{1,\dots,L\}$; a first-order schedule
uses the same permutation $\pi$ every step. We first record that the gate budget is
a schedule invariant for the schedules we compare at matched cost.

\begin{proposition}[Rotation-count invariance]
\label{prop:cost}
A first-order schedule with any ordering $\pi$, and the antithetic schedule that
alternates $\pi$ with its reverse, each apply exactly $L\cdot r$ single-Pauli
rotations for $r$ steps. Hence their rotation count is independent of the ordering
and of the alternation.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
One first-order step is an ordered product of exactly one factor per term, $L$
rotations; $r$ steps give $L\cdot r$. The antithetic schedule applies, at each step,
a permutation of the same $L$ factors (forward on even steps, reversed on odd), so
each step is again $L$ rotations and the total is $L\cdot r$; reversing a step's
order permutes its factors without adding or removing any.
\end{proof}

The next lemma pins the exact value of the Pauli commutators that populate the
error form.

\begin{lemma}[Pauli commutator value]
\label{lem:commvalue}
Let $h_j=c_jP_j$, $h_k=c_kP_k$ with real $c_j,c_k$. If $P_j,P_k$ commute then
$[h_j,h_k]=0$. If they anticommute then $[h_j,h_k]=2c_jc_k\,P_jP_k=2c_jc_k\,\omega_{jk}R_{jk}$
with $\omega_{jk}\in\{\pm\ii\}$ and $R_{jk}$ a Hermitian Pauli string, so
$\norm{[h_j,h_k]}_{\mathrm{HS}}=2\abs{c_jc_k}\,2^{n/2}$ and the operator norm is
$2\abs{c_jc_k}$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
By Lemma~\ref{lem:parity}, $P_kP_j=(-1)^{k(P_j,P_k)}P_jP_k$. If they commute,
$[h_j,h_k]=c_jc_k(P_jP_k-P_kP_j)=0$. If they anticommute, $P_kP_j=-P_jP_k$, so
$[h_j,h_k]=2c_jc_k\,P_jP_k$. The product $P_jP_k=\omega_{jk}R_{jk}$ is a phased Pauli
(Sec.~\ref{sec:pauli}); since the commutator of Hermitians is anti-Hermitian and
$R_{jk}$ is Hermitian, $\omega_{jk}\in\{\pm\ii\}$. Every Pauli string is unitary with
$\norm{R_{jk}}=1$ and $\norm{R_{jk}}_{\mathrm{HS}}^2=\Tr(R_{jk}^2)=2^n$, giving the
stated norms.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Commutator error form]
\label{def:errorform}
For an ordering $\pi$, the leading error operator is $E_\pi=\sum_{a<b}[h_{\pi(a)},
h_{\pi(b)}]$. By Lemma~\ref{lem:commvalue} it equals the edge sum
Eq.~\eqref{eq:edgesum}, a signed sum of phased Pauli strings indexed by the edges of
$\calG(H)$, where $\varepsilon_{jk}(\pi)=+1$ if $j$ precedes $k$ in $\pi$ and $-1$
otherwise. It is assembled in $O(L^2 n)$ time without forming a $2^n\times2^n$
matrix, and is validated against the dense pair-commutator sum to $10^{-9}$ (the
\texttt{error\_form\_verified} flag).
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Ordering impotence]
\label{thm:impotence}
Let $H=\sum_j c_jP_j$ and let $E_\pi$ be its commutator error form. Reordering acts
on $E_\pi$ only by flipping the signs $\varepsilon_{jk}$; the multiset of pair
commutators $\{[h_j,h_k]\}$ is independent of $\pi$. Consequently, grouping the edge
sum by Pauli string,
\[
\norm{E_\pi}_{\mathrm{HS}}^2
=2^n\sum_{R}\Bigl(\sum_{\{j,k\}:R_{jk}=R}\varepsilon_{jk}(\pi)\,2c_jc_k\,\widehat\omega_{jk}\Bigr)^{\!2},
\]
where $\widehat\omega_{jk}=\omega_{jk}/\ii\in\{\pm1\}$. If every edge maps to a
distinct Pauli string $R$ (no collisions), then $\norm{E_\pi}_{\mathrm{HS}}^2=2^n\sum_{\{j,k\}}(2c_jc_k)^2$
is \emph{independent of $\pi$}. In general only the colliding edges contribute an
ordering-dependent term, so $\norm{E_\pi}_{\mathrm{HS}}$ is bounded below by the
ordering-invariant contribution of the non-colliding edges for \emph{every} $\pi$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For an unordered pair $\{j,k\}$, listing $j$ before $k$ contributes $[h_j,h_k]$ and
listing $k$ before $j$ contributes $[h_k,h_j]=-[h_j,h_k]$; hence reordering replaces
the contribution by its negative, i.e.\ multiplies it by $\varepsilon_{jk}(\pi)$,
and never removes or adds a pair. By Lemma~\ref{lem:commvalue} each anticommuting
edge contributes $2c_jc_k\,\omega_{jk}R_{jk}=\ii\,2c_jc_k\,\widehat\omega_{jk}R_{jk}$.
Distinct Pauli strings are Hilbert--Schmidt orthogonal with squared norm $2^n$, so
$\norm{E_\pi}_{\mathrm{HS}}^2=2^n\sum_R\abs{\alpha_R(\pi)}^2$ with
$\alpha_R(\pi)=\ii\sum_{\{j,k\}:R_{jk}=R}\varepsilon_{jk}(\pi)2c_jc_k\widehat\omega_{jk}$,
which is the displayed expression. If each $R$ has a single contributing edge then
$\abs{\alpha_R}=2\abs{c_jc_k}$ regardless of the sign $\varepsilon_{jk}$, so the sum
is $\pi$-independent. Edges that are alone on their Pauli string therefore
contribute a fixed amount to $\norm{E_\pi}_{\mathrm{HS}}^2$ for every $\pi$; the
remaining (colliding) edges are the only source of ordering dependence, and their
contribution is non-negative, giving the stated lower bound.
\end{proof}

Theorem~\ref{thm:impotence} is the precise content of ``ordering is within noise'':
on the transverse-field Ising and Heisenberg families every anticommuting edge maps
to a distinct Pauli string (Table~\ref{tab:impotence}, ordering-invariant fraction
$1.00$), so $\norm{E_\pi}_{\mathrm{HS}}$ is \emph{exactly} the same for every
ordering, and on the molecular-like family only a small colliding fraction is
reducible. We now show that enlarging from orderings to schedules escapes this
floor.

\begin{theorem}[Antithetic cancellation and the free order upgrade]
\label{thm:antithetic}
Fix an ordering $\pi$ and step size $\Delta=t/r$ with $r$ even, and let
$S_\pi(\Delta)=\prod_{a=1}^{L}e^{-\ii\Delta h_{\pi(a)}}$ and
$S_{\pi^R}(\Delta)=\prod_{a=L}^{1}e^{-\ii\Delta h_{\pi(a)}}$ be the forward and
reversed steps. The antithetic step-pair $M(\Delta)=S_{\pi^R}(\Delta)S_\pi(\Delta)$
is symmetric, $M(-\Delta)=M(\Delta)^{-1}$, so its generator contains only odd powers
of $\Delta$:
\[
M(\Delta)=\exp\!\bigl(-2\ii\Delta H+O(\Delta^3)\bigr),
\]
and the leading $O(\Delta^2)$ commutator error $-\tfrac{\Delta^2}{2}E_\pi$ cancels.
Consequently the antithetic schedule $\bigl(S_{\pi^R}(\Delta)S_\pi(\Delta)\bigr)^{r/2}$
approximates $e^{-\ii tH}$ with additive error $O(t^3/r^2)$, while applying exactly
$L\cdot r$ rotations---the identical budget of the first-order schedule
(Proposition~\ref{prop:cost}).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Write the factors of $M(\Delta)$ left to right: $e^{-\ii\Delta h_{\pi(L)}},\dots,
e^{-\ii\Delta h_{\pi(1)}},e^{-\ii\Delta h_{\pi(1)}},\dots,e^{-\ii\Delta h_{\pi(L)}}$,
a palindromic sequence. Reversing the order of the factors and replacing each by its
inverse (i.e.\ $\Delta\mapsto-\Delta$) returns the same product read backwards, so
$M(-\Delta)=M(\Delta)^{-1}$. Writing $M(\Delta)=\exp(\Omega(\Delta))$ with
$\Omega(\Delta)=\sum_{m\ge1}\Omega_m\Delta^m$ (Baker--Campbell--Hausdorff, convergent
for small $\Delta$), the relation $M(-\Delta)=M(\Delta)^{-1}$ reads
$\exp(\Omega(-\Delta))=\exp(-\Omega(\Delta))$, i.e.\ $\Omega(-\Delta)=-\Omega(\Delta)$,
so all even-order terms $\Omega_{2m}$ vanish; in particular the $\Delta^2$ term,
which for the ordered product $\prod_a e^{-\ii\Delta h_a}$ equals
$-\tfrac{1}{2}\sum_{a<b}[h_a,h_b]$ before symmetrisation, is removed. Each term $h_j$
appears twice in $M$, once in each half, so $\Omega_1=-2\ii H$ and
$M(\Delta)=\exp(-2\ii\Delta H+O(\Delta^3))$. Thus one antithetic pair is a
second-order approximant of $e^{-2\ii\Delta H}$; composing $r/2$ pairs with
$\Delta=t/r$ telescopes (as in the standard product-formula bound) to additive error
$r/2\cdot O(\Delta^3)=O(t^3/r^2)$. The pair applies $2L$ rotations and there are
$r/2$ pairs, giving $L\cdot r$ rotations in total.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Clique exactness]
\label{prop:colour}
A proper colouring of $\calG(H)$ partitions the terms into cliques of pairwise
commuting Pauli terms. Laying a clique $C$ out contiguously in the schedule, its
rotations satisfy $\prod_{j\in C}e^{-\ii\Delta h_j}=\exp(-\ii\Delta\sum_{j\in C}h_j)$
exactly for any internal order, so no first-order error arises among the members of a
clique; the schedule error depends only on inter-clique commutators. The number of
non-commuting units is reduced from $L$ to the number of cliques, which is at least
the chromatic number $\chi(\calG)$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
In a proper colouring no two adjacent vertices share a colour, and adjacency in
$\calG(H)$ is anticommutation, so same-colour terms commute. For pairwise commuting
$\{h_j\}_{j\in C}$ the exponentials factor exactly, $\prod_{j\in C}e^{-\ii\Delta h_j}
=\exp(-\ii\Delta\sum_{j\in C}h_j)$, independent of internal order; hence by
Lemma~\ref{lem:commvalue} no intra-clique pair contributes to $E_\pi$, and only
inter-clique edges remain.
\end{proof}

For low-chromatic Hamiltonians (e.g.\ Heisenberg, where the bond operators colour
into a few commuting classes) a clique-ordered first-order schedule is therefore
near-exact at a single step, which is why folding adds only overhead there
(Sec.~\ref{sec:family}).

\subsection{The learned router}
\label{sec:router_method}
Ten listing-order-invariant features of $\calG(H)$ are computed per instance: term
count; anticommutation (edge) density; mean and maximum node degree; number of
greedy colour cliques; largest-clique fraction; coefficient coefficient-of-variation;
mean Pauli weight; and two commutator-error-form features---the colliding fraction
and the ordering-invariant fraction (Definition~\ref{def:errorform}). A standardised
\texttt{LogisticRegression} classifier~\cite{pedregosa2011scikit} maps these to the
label ``which fixed schedule reaches the target fidelity at the fewest rotations on
this instance,'' trained leave-one-instance-out (each test instance scored by a
classifier trained only on the other $\Ninstances-1$ instances), falling back to the
symmetric schedule when training labels are single-class.

\begin{proposition}[Listing-order invariance of the router]
\label{prop:invariance}
Let $\sigma\in S_L$ relabel the terms of $H$. Each of the ten features is a function
of the unordered weighted graph $\calG(H)$ and is invariant under $\sigma$.
Consequently the router's predicted schedule depends only on the isomorphism class
of $\calG(H)$, not on how the terms were listed.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Relabeling maps $\calG(H)$ to an isomorphic graph with the same coefficient multiset.
Vertex/edge counts, the degree multiset, the greedy-colouring clique counts (under a
deterministic degree-driven tie-break), the coefficient coefficient-of-variation, and
the mean Pauli weight are graph isomorphism invariants. The colliding and
ordering-invariant fractions count, over unordered anticommuting pairs, how the
products $P_jP_k$ coincide---a symmetric function of the unordered term multiset
(Definition~\ref{def:errorform}). Each feature is therefore invariant under $\sigma$,
so the standardised classifier's prediction is too.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Hamiltonian families and protocol}
\label{sec:families_method}
Three families are generated as $\{(c_j,P_j)\}$ term lists: transverse-field Ising
$H=-J\sum_i Z_iZ_{i+1}-h\sum_i X_i$; Heisenberg $H=\sum_i(J_x X_iX_{i+1}+J_y
Y_iY_{i+1}+J_z Z_iZ_{i+1})$; and \emph{molecular-like}, random local 2-body Pauli
terms with seeded Gaussian coefficients (a tractable stand-in for the dense,
irregular anticommutation structure of Jordan--Wigner / Bravyi--Kitaev mapped
electronic-structure Hamiltonians~\cite{bravyi2002fermionic,whitfield2011simulation}).
The reported-scale configuration (\texttt{configs/full.yaml}) uses sizes
$n\in\{4,5,6,7,8\}$, eight seeded instances per (family, size), evolution time
$t=\EvolTime$, a reference budget of $r=\RefSteps$ steps, and a step grid
$\{1,2,4,6,8,12,16,24\}$ for the convergence and frontier curves, giving
$\Ninstances$ instances. For each instance and schedule we evaluate the product
formula, score fidelity and infidelity against the exact reference, and record the
realised rotation count. From the (rotation count, fidelity) curves we compute the
rotations to reach each target fidelity (the matched-cost efficiency metric) and the
fitted convergence rate; ordering impotence is measured by sampling $48$ orderings
per instance and forming the exact leading-error spectral norm. Per-schedule means
carry 95\% confidence intervals $1.96\,\hat\sigma/\sqrt{N}$.

\section{The problem in one paragraph}
\label{si:problem}
We want to simulate $e^{-\ii H t}\ket{\psi_0}$. On a quantum computer we approximate
$e^{-\ii H t}$ by a \emph{product formula} that chains together easy one-term
rotations. The cost of a first-order schedule is one rotation per term per step, and
this cost does not depend on the order in which the rotations are applied---nor on
whether that order \emph{varies between steps}. The error does. The scientific
question is which use of this freedom reduces the error: we show a single fixed order
cannot (Sec.~\ref{si:impotence}), while a step-alternating \emph{schedule} can, for
free (Sec.~\ref{si:antithetic}).

\section{Hamiltonians as Pauli sums}
\label{si:hamiltonians}
\emph{Source:} \texttt{src/topoham/hamiltonians.py}. Every Hamiltonian on $n$ qubits
is a real combination of Pauli strings, $H=\sum_{j}c_jP_j$, each $P_j$ a length-$n$
word over $\{I,X,Y,Z\}$. Three families are generated as term lists: transverse-field
Ising, Heisenberg, and a molecular-like family of random local two-body terms with
seeded Gaussian coefficients---spanning sparse-and-fast-forwardable to
dense-and-stubborn anticommutation structure.

\section{Pauli algebra: three facts that make exact simulation cheap}
\label{si:pauli}
\emph{Source:} \texttt{src/topoham/pauli.py}. (A) Two Pauli strings commute iff they
anticommute on an even number of qubits---an $O(n)$ parity test
(Lemma~\ref{lem:parity}). (B) A non-identity Pauli is an involution, so
$e^{-\ii\theta P}\ket{\psi}=\cos\theta\,\ket{\psi}-\ii\sin\theta\,(P\ket{\psi})$ is a
single sparse application of $P$ plus a scaled add (Lemma~\ref{lem:rotation}); this is
the inner kernel of the simulator, and the in-browser demo applies the same identity.
(C) The product of two Pauli strings is a phased Pauli, $PQ=\omega R$ with
$\omega\in\{\pm1,\pm\ii\}$ (\texttt{pauli\_product})---the algebra behind the
commutator error form. Before any fidelity is produced, the fast kernel is checked
against dense \texttt{expm} to $10^{-9}$ (\texttt{pauli\_algebra\_verified}).

\section{The commutator graph and the error form}
\label{si:graph}
\emph{Source:} \texttt{src/topoham/commutator\_graph.py},
\texttt{src/topoham/error\_form.py}. The graph $\calG(H)$ has one node per term and an
edge between anticommuting pairs. Its proper colouring partitions the terms into
mutually commuting cliques (Proposition~\ref{prop:colour}), each exponentiable exactly
and simultaneously. The \emph{commutator error form} (Definition~\ref{def:errorform})
is the exact leading error operator $E_\pi=\sum_{a<b}[h_{\pi(a)},h_{\pi(b)}]$, built
matrix-free as a signed sum of phased Pauli strings over the edges. The module also
computes the ten listing-order-invariant features the learned router reads, including
the colliding and ordering-invariant fractions, and validates the matrix-free form
against the dense pair-commutator sum to $10^{-9}$ (\texttt{error\_form\_verified}).

\section{Why ordering is impotent (the theorem, derived)}
\label{si:impotence}
\emph{Source: Methods, Sec.~\ref{sec:graph_method}.} For Pauli terms the only
ordering-dependent feature of $E_\pi$ is the sign $\varepsilon_{jk}$ of each edge's
commutator: listing $j$ before $k$ gives $[h_j,h_k]$, the reverse gives its negative.
Because distinct Pauli strings are Hilbert--Schmidt orthogonal, the squared HS norm of
$E_\pi$ is a sum over Pauli strings of squared coefficients; an edge alone on its
Pauli string contributes a fixed amount regardless of its sign. Hence on families
with no colliding edges $\norm{E_\pi}_{\mathrm{HS}}$ is exactly the same for every
ordering, and in general only the colliding fraction is reducible
(Theorem~\ref{thm:impotence}). Empirically the spectral norm of a random ordering is
within a factor $\ImpMedian$ of the per-instance minimum: ordering cannot escape the
$O(t^2/r)$ rate.

\section{Why a schedule escapes it (the cancellation theorem)}
\label{si:antithetic}
\emph{Source:} \texttt{src/topoham/schedules.py}. A \emph{schedule} may apply a
different order at each step. The \emph{antithetic} schedule alternates an order $\pi$
with its reverse $\pi^R$; each adjacent step-pair $S_{\pi^R}(\Delta)S_\pi(\Delta)$
reads as a palindrome, so it is a symmetric formula whose generator has only odd
powers of $\Delta$, cancelling the $O(\Delta^2)$ commutator error
(Theorem~\ref{thm:antithetic}). The result is second-order accuracy, $O(t^3/r^2)$, at
exactly $L\cdot r$ rotations---the same as first-order. The \emph{symmetric} (Strang)
schedule applies each term twice per step at half angle with seam merges, a
lower-constant second-order fold at $\approx2L\cdot r$ rotations. Both fold over the
clique order so that intra-clique error is already absent (Proposition~\ref{prop:colour}).

\section{Schedules, the matched-cost metric, and the learned router}
\label{si:policies}
\emph{Source:} \texttt{src/topoham/schedules.py}, \texttt{policies.py},
\texttt{env.py}. A schedule is realised as a flat list of (term, step-fraction)
rotations; its (seam-merged) length is the gate cost on which all schedules are
compared. For each instance and schedule and each step count $r$ in the grid, the
environment builds the exact reference $e^{-\ii Ht}\ket{+}^{\otimes n}$ and scores
fidelity, and from the resulting (cost, fidelity) curve we read the rotations to a
target fidelity. The learned router (\texttt{LearnedSchedulePolicy}) is a standardised
logistic-regression classifier over the ten graph features that predicts the
gate-optimal fixed schedule, trained leave-one-instance-out
(Sec.~\ref{sec:router_method}); it returns the predicted schedule, a selector over
human-readable schedules rather than a black box, and its decision is listing-order
invariant (Proposition~\ref{prop:invariance}).

\section{Metrics, aggregation, and provenance}
\label{si:metrics}
\emph{Source:} \texttt{metrics.py}, \texttt{runner.py}, \texttt{make\_tables.py},
\texttt{make\_figures.py}. Per schedule we report the mean fidelity with a 95\%
confidence interval $1.96\,\hat\sigma/\sqrt{N}$, the convergence rate (mean
per-instance log--log slope of infidelity vs.\ $r$), and the rotations to each target
fidelity, overall and per family. \texttt{runner.run} writes
\texttt{results/summary.json}---the single source of truth---with full provenance and
the two integrity flags. The manuscript \texttt{\textbackslash input}'s the generated
macros and table fragments and reads the figures from \texttt{code/figures/}, so
nothing is typed by hand. An audit (\texttt{make audit}) checks that the headline
macros are present, the integrity flags are set, and no forbidden superiority phrasing
appears.

\section{How to reproduce everything}
\label{si:reproduce}
From \texttt{submission/code}:
\begin{verbatim}
pip install .                         # installs the topoham package
make test                             # Pauli kernel (1e-9), error-form (1e-9),
                                      # antithetic 2nd-order, clique exactness
export KMP_DUPLICATE_LIB_OK=TRUE
topoham-reproduce --config configs/full.yaml   # summary.json, tables, figures
\end{verbatim}
The reported run (seed $0$): $\Ninstances$ instances ($3$ families $\times$ $5$ sizes
$\times$ $8$ seeded draws), time $t=\EvolTime$, reference budget $r=\RefSteps$, step
grid $\{1,2,4,6,8,12,16,24\}$. Headline: the antithetic schedule reaches fidelity
$0.99$ at $\GtAnti$ rotations against $\GtFirst$ for the best first-order ordering
(a $\GateSpeedup\times$ reduction) and $0.999$ at $\GtAntiHard$ rotations where
first-order Trotter does not reach it, lifting the convergence rate from $\SlopeFirst$
to $\SlopeAnti$ at the identical gate budget.
